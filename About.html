WAYS OF IPROVING MEDICAL ADHERENCE FOR PATIENTS

The problem of non-adherence to medical treatment remains a challenge for the medical professions and social scientists.

PATIENT EDUCATION
A successful educational intervention may require participation from both the physician and patient. Admittedly, some patients may wish to entrust most of the decision making to their treating physician. It is, therefore, crucial to determine how involved the patient wishes to be in constructing the management plan (Harnett, Wiederkehr, Gerber, Gruben, & Koenig, 2016). As soon as this is established, a proper educational intervention could involve intertwining biomedical information into pre-existing lay belief systems. Patient education about the disease and its treatment by primary care physicians can help to improve adherence to medication (Joplin, Van Der Zwan, Joshua, & Wong, 2015)

ELCTRONIC REMINDERS
An electronic reminder is an automatically sent reminder without personal contact between healthcare provider and patient. Patients who are adherent at first can become non-adherent over time. This may be the reason that (Pop-Eleches, Thirumurthy, & Habyarimana, 2011) found out that reminders can especially provide a solution for patients who are unintentionally non-adherent, i.e., patients who are willing to take their medication but forget it or are inaccurate with their timing. Forgetfulness is commonly reported as a barrier to adherence in various patient populations. (Haynes, Ackloo, & Sahota , 2008) Shows the range of patients reporting this barrier varies from 22% to 73% across the studies, in all studies forgetting to take a dose was the most frequently cited reason for nonadherence. Previous reviews evaluating strategies for improving adherence among which electronic reminders often focused on specific patient populations. For example, (van Dijk, Bouvy, Linn, Van Weert, & De Bakker, 2012) found that a reminder device might be beneficial to patients with glaucoma and that half of the studies included in their review reported significantly improved adherence in HIV patients as a result of Electronic Reminders.

SIMLE MEDICATION LABELS
In order for a patient to adhere fully to the instructions for a prescribed medicine, it is essential that, at the very least, the instructions are understood, can be acted on and can be recalled. One way of facilitating the communication of medicine information to patients with limited literacy is to incorporate visual aids such as pictograms on medicine labels and in patient information leaflets. Pictograms have been shown to enhance comprehension and recall of information when used both alone and in combination with text (Dowse & Martina, 2005). The interpretation of a wide range of pictograms has been assessed in participants from a number of different South African language groups all of whom displayed limited reading skills. These papers include comment on practical application as well as discussion on the misinterpretation of the pictograms.
